## (1) Building Products for the Enterprise — Blair Reeves and Benjamin Gaines

A unique book that starts off on a strong note, defining the expected role of a product manager and describing precisely (how + why) building products for an *enterprise* is fundamentally different from building directly with the consumer in mind.

One key takeaway for me was understanding how in my previous roles I had often spent hours obsessing over UI elements, color, layout — when the user may not even be our paying consumer in this case! This insight is developed further in the preceding chapters developing a clear distinction between the **customer** and the **user**.

Gently the readers are led to concepts of product roadmaps, vision, metrics and each section concludes with a short, insightful interview with an existing product manager from an enterprise.

So, how does one drive the team as a PM? The authors posit having a sound grasp of three kinds of knowledge: **Organization, Product and Industry** — it is surprising to note that business knowledge was not mentioned as a requirement.

There is a brief *Do's and Don'ts* list of communication and team strategies, a few that stick:

- Don't blame an internal team
- Be honest and forthright about your product limitations

Solid advice. This chapter manages to drill into our minds that the product manager's focus should be on **customer problems**, rather than implementing solutions.

One of my favourite chapters is that of data-enabled decisions. The authors list out various sources for a PM to consider before evaluating game-changing decisions.

Personally as a fledgling aspirant, this honest line made a mark:

> "Product management should not be a first gig in the software industry — this is a highly cross-functional role, which benefits greatly from using prior domain experience."

**My rating: 8/10** — It fundamentally assumes a respectable understanding of your knowledge base and proceeds to dish out practical, high-level advice in short nuggets.
